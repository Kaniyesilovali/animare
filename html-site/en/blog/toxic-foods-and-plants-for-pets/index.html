Toxic Foods and Plants for Dogs and Cats: Hidden Dangers at Home

10 July 2026 · Animare Veterinary

Most poisoning cases happen not on the street but inside the home: a piece of chocolate snatched from the kitchen counter, a grape dropped on the floor, or the potted plant in the living room. In this guide we summarise, from a veterinarian's perspective, the riskiest foods and plants for dogs and cats, the symptoms of poisoning, and the right first steps.

Foods Toxic to Dogs

  • Chocolate and cocoa: The theobromine they contain can cause heart rhythm disturbances and seizures. The higher the cocoa content (dark chocolate, cocoa powder), the greater the danger.
  • Grapes and raisins: Can cause acute kidney failure; there is no safe amount.
  • Onion, garlic, leek: Raw, cooked or powdered, they destroy red blood cells and cause anaemia.
  • Xylitol (sweetener): Found in sugar-free gum, some peanut butters and diet products. Can cause a sudden drop in blood sugar and liver failure — one of the most dangerous substances for dogs.
  • Macadamia nuts: Cause weakness, tremors and hind-leg wobbliness.
  • Alcohol and raw yeast dough: Dough ferments in the stomach, causing both alcohol poisoning and dangerous bloating.
  • Coffee and energy drinks: Caffeine affects the heart and nervous system much like theobromine.
  • Avocado: The persin it contains can cause digestive upset, and the stone poses an obstruction risk.

Foods Toxic to Cats

Most of the list above applies to cats as well; the onion-garlic family and chocolate cause serious poisoning in cats too. Additional cat-specific risks:

  • Raw fish and raw meat (fed regularly): Carry a risk of thiamine deficiency and bacterial infection.
  • Milk and dairy products: Most adult cats cannot digest lactose, which leads to diarrhoea. Contrary to popular belief, milk is not a suitable drink for cats.
  • Tinned tuna for humans (fed regularly): As a sole diet it creates serious nutritional imbalance.

Toxic Plants: In the Living Room and the Garden

  • Lilies (Lilium): Among the most lethal plants for cats. Leaves, pollen, even the vase water can cause acute kidney failure. Lilies should never enter a home with cats.
  • Oleander: Extremely common along roadsides and in gardens across North Cyprus; every part of it is toxic to the heart. In homes with gardens, make sure dogs don't chew it.
  • Sago palm (Cycas): A popular ornamental in Mediterranean gardens; its seeds in particular cause severe liver failure in dogs.
  • Tulip and hyacinth bulbs: A risk for dogs that dig in the soil.
  • Dieffenbachia, monstera, pothos: Cause burning in the mouth, excessive drooling and difficulty swallowing.
  • Azalea and many ornamental flowers: If in doubt about any plant, keep it out of your pet's reach.

Symptoms of Poisoning

Symptoms vary by substance, but suspect poisoning if you see any of the following:

  • Sudden vomiting, diarrhoea or excessive drooling
  • Tremors, unsteady walking, seizures
  • Extreme restlessness or, conversely, sudden lethargy
  • Pale or bluish gums, rapid breathing
  • Excessive thirst and urination changes (especially after grapes or lilies)

Some of these signs overlap with other emergencies — you can find the full picture in our post on the 10 signs that mean an immediate vet visit.

If You Suspect Poisoning: Do's and Don'ts

  • Do: Note what was eaten, when, and roughly how much; take the packaging or a piece of the plant with you and get to a clinic without delay.
  • Don't: Induce vomiting without veterinary guidance — with corrosive substances or a drowsy animal it makes things worse.
  • Don't: Use home "antidotes" such as milk, salt water or oil; they have no scientific basis and waste time.
  • Don't: Wait it out because "it was only a little" — with substances like grapes and xylitol even small amounts are critical.

In poisoning cases the single most important factor is elapsed time. Intervention in the first hours (induced vomiting, activated charcoal, fluid support) lets most patients get through with a mild course.

Prevention in Short

Choose a bin with a lid, don't leave food on the counter, ask guests not to feed from the table, and check the species before bringing a new plant home. If your pet has eaten something suspicious or shows any of the signs above, get in touch with our clinic right away.

Frequently Asked Questions

My dog ate chocolate — what should I do?

Note the type of chocolate (dark or milk), the estimated amount and your dog's weight, and get to a veterinary clinic without delay. Dark chocolate and high-cocoa products are far more dangerous; acting early, before symptoms appear, greatly improves the outcome.

Is a small amount of grapes or raisins really harmful?

There is no known safe dose for grapes and raisins; in some dogs even a few can cause acute kidney failure. If your dog has eaten grapes, consult a veterinarian regardless of the amount.

Which plant is the most dangerous for cats?

Lilies (Lilium species) are among the most lethal plants for cats. Every part — leaves, flowers, pollen, even the vase water — can cause acute kidney failure. Lilies should not be kept in a household with cats.

Can I make my pet vomit at home after poisoning?

No — inducing vomiting without veterinary guidance can make things worse. It is dangerous with corrosive substances and in animals with reduced consciousness. Home remedies such as salt, milk or oil are also harmful; the right step is to reach a clinic quickly.
