Tick-Borne Diseases in Dogs: Ehrlichia and Babesia

18 Jul 2026 · Animare Veterinary

Why a Tick Is More Than Just a Parasite

A tick is an external parasite that feeds on a dog's blood — but the real danger is not the bite itself, it is the diseases the tick carries. As it feeds, a tick can pass the microorganisms inside it into the dog's bloodstream. Cyprus's warm climate keeps tick populations active almost year-round, which makes tick-borne diseases one of the most common health problems we see in our region.

The two diseases we encounter most often are Ehrlichiosis and Babesiosis. Both are carried by the brown dog tick (Rhipicephalus sanguineus).

Ehrlichiosis (Ehrlichia canis)

The Ehrlichia bacterium targets the dog's white blood cells and the platelets that allow blood to clot. The disease progresses insidiously and can become chronic if untreated. Common signs include:

  • Fever, lethargy, and loss of appetite
  • Nosebleeds and easy bruising of the skin (due to low platelets)
  • Weight loss
  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Eye problems (redness, cloudy vision)
  • Joint pain and lameness

Babesiosis (Babesia)

The Babesia parasite invades the red blood cells directly and destroys them, causing a rapidly developing anaemia. Common signs include:

  • Pale or yellowish gums (a sign of anaemia and jaundice)
  • Lethargy and marked weakness
  • Fever
  • Dark, brown, or reddish urine
  • Loss of appetite and rapid breathing

Besides these two, other tick-borne agents such as Anaplasma and Hepatozoon can also occur in our region. Because the signs often overlap, a blood test is needed to tell them apart.

How to Remove a Tick Correctly

When you find a tick on your dog, acting quickly and correctly lowers the risk:

  • Place a tick-removal hook or fine-tipped tweezers as close to the skin as possible.
  • Pull slowly with a steady, straight motion, without twisting.
  • Do not crush or squeeze the tick — this forces its stomach contents into the dog.
  • Do not apply alcohol, cologne, oil, or fire; these methods increase the risk of transmission.
  • After removal, clean the area and watch your dog for signs over the following weeks.

Prevention: The Best Treatment Is Protection

With tick-borne diseases too, prevention is far easier than treatment:

  • Regular preventives: Use vet-approved tick collars, spot-on drops, or oral tablets consistently, without gaps.
  • Daily checks: After every walk, run your hands over the ears, neck, armpits, and between the toes.
  • Garden care: Keep long grass trimmed; ticks favour damp, shady undergrowth.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis is made by examining a blood sample; rapid test kits and a blood count give an accurate diagnosis. Dogs diagnosed early usually respond well to treatment. Ehrlichiosis is treated with appropriate antibiotics, and Babesiosis with specific anti-parasitic medication. The key is to notice the signs early and see a vet without delay — because in advanced cases treatment becomes much harder.

Frequently Asked Questions

I found a tick on my dog — should I go to the vet right away?

It is important to remove the tick correctly as soon as possible, because the longer a tick stays attached, the higher the risk of disease transmission. If in the weeks after removal your dog shows signs such as lethargy, loss of appetite, or fever, see your vet. Some diseases only appear weeks later.

How should I remove a tick at home?

Using a tick-removal hook or fine-tipped tweezers, grip the tick as close to the skin as possible and pull it out with a steady, straight motion without twisting. Do not crush or squeeze the tick, and do not apply alcohol, cologne, oil, or fire — these cause the tick to empty its stomach contents into the dog and increase the risk of transmission.

Can tick-borne diseases spread from dogs to humans?

Ehrlichiosis and Babesiosis do not spread from dog to human by direct contact. However, the same ticks can bite people too, so tick control matters for the whole family's health.

How do I protect my dog from ticks?

Use vet-approved tick collars, spot-on drops, or oral tablet preventives regularly. Also check your dog's ears, neck, armpits, and between the toes by hand after every walk. Regular prevention combined with daily checks gives the best result.
