Leishmaniasis in Dogs: Why It Matters So Much in Cyprus

18 Jul 2026 · Animare Veterinary

What Is Leishmaniasis?

Leishmaniasis is a serious disease caused by a single-celled parasite called Leishmania. In dogs it is a chronic, progressive infection that affects the skin, internal organs, and immune system, and it can be fatal if left untreated. It is endemic across the Mediterranean basin, including North Cyprus — meaning it is constantly present in our region.

Cyprus's warm climate and long summer create ideal conditions for the flies that carry the disease. That is why it is so important for every dog owner to recognise this illness and understand how to prevent it.

How Does It Spread?

Leishmaniasis does not spread directly from dog to dog by contact. Transmission happens only through the bite of an infected sandfly (Phlebotomus). Much smaller than a mosquito, these flies are almost silent and are most active from dusk until dawn.

When a sandfly bites an infected dog it picks up the parasite, then passes it on to the next healthy dog it bites. This cycle is at its highest risk between late spring and early autumn, when the local sandfly population is at its peak.

Signs to Watch For

Signs can appear months or even years after infection and progress slowly. The most common include:

  • Hair loss and flaky skin around the eyes (a "spectacle" look), on the nose, and at the ear tips
  • Skin sores and crusting that do not heal
  • Progressive weight loss and muscle wasting despite a normal appetite
  • Lethargy, reluctance to move, and tiring easily
  • Abnormally long, curling, brittle nails
  • Nosebleeds
  • Swollen lymph nodes (glands)
  • Increased thirst and urination (which can signal kidney involvement)

Why Is It So Dangerous?

Leishmaniasis progresses insidiously. Early on the signs may be mild or unnoticeable, but during this time the parasite damages internal organs — above all the kidneys. In untreated dogs the most common cause of death is kidney failure. That is why regular blood checks and early diagnosis are vital for dogs living in at-risk areas.

Prevention: The Most Effective Path

With Leishmaniasis, prevention is far easier and more valuable than treatment. For the best result, combine these measures:

  • Repellent collars and spot-on drops: Vet-approved products that repel sandflies before they bite are your first line of defence. We cover why ectoparasite protection is vital in a separate article.
  • Vaccination: Discuss the Leishmania vaccine with your vet; it adds protection for at-risk dogs.
  • Timing: Keep your dog indoors as much as possible from dusk to dawn, when sandflies are most active.
  • Physical barriers: Fit fine mesh screens on windows and doors.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosis is made by testing a blood sample for antibodies and the presence of the parasite. In most dogs the disease cannot be completely eliminated; but in dogs diagnosed early, appropriate medication and regular monitoring keep the parasite under control, and the dog can live comfortably for many years. Treatment is a lifelong management process.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Leishmaniasis spread from dogs to humans?

The disease does not spread through direct contact with a dog; transmission requires the bite of an infected sandfly. However, because infected dogs act as a reservoir for local sandflies, there is an indirect risk for people with weakened immune systems. This is why prevention matters for both your dog and your family.

Can Leishmaniasis be completely cured?

In most dogs the parasite cannot be fully cleared from the body; it is a chronic condition managed for life. However, with early diagnosis and regular treatment, dogs can live healthy, comfortable lives for many years. This is why prevention is far more valuable than treatment.

How do I protect my dog from Leishmaniasis?

Use vet-approved sandfly-repellent collars or spot-on drops, keep your dog indoors as much as possible between dusk and dawn when sandflies are most active, fit fine mesh screens on windows, and discuss the Leishmania vaccine with your vet. Used together, these measures provide the strongest protection.

Can cats get Leishmaniasis too?

It is far rarer in cats but not impossible. The disease mainly affects dogs. Even so, cats can develop skin lesions, so a veterinary assessment is advised in suspicious cases.
