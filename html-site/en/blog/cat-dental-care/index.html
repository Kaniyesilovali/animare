Cat Dental Care: Why It Matters More Than You Think

28 May 2026 · Animare Veterinary

Why Take Your Cat's Dental Health Seriously?

Research shows that the vast majority of cats over the age of three have some degree of dental disease. Untreated tartar and gum inflammation allow bacteria to enter the bloodstream, potentially damaging vital organs such as the heart, kidneys, and liver.

Signs of Dental Problems

See your vet if you notice any of the following:

  • Bad breath (halitosis)
  • Yellow or brown tartar build-up
  • Red or bleeding gums
  • Tilting the head or eating slowly
  • Loss of appetite

Professional Dental Care

At our clinic, ultrasonic tartar cleaning is performed under short-acting anaesthesia, ensuring the cat remains stress-free throughout the procedure. Gum health is assessed after cleaning, and damaged teeth are extracted where necessary.

Home Dental Care Tips

  • Use a soft toothbrush designed for cats and enzymatic toothpaste (never use human toothpaste — it is toxic to cats).
  • Start by letting your cat lick the paste off your finger, then gradually introduce the brush.
  • Dental-support diets and chew toys can also help maintain oral hygiene between professional cleans.

How Often Should I Have My Cat's Teeth Cleaned?

Professional cleaning at least once a year is recommended. Breeds prone to tartar may benefit from more frequent appointments. Contact us to book.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my cat has tartar?

Yellow-brown buildup on teeth, bad breath, red gums, or difficulty chewing are signs of tartar. In this case, a veterinary check-up is recommended.

Can cat dental cleaning be done without anesthesia?

Effective ultrasonic tartar removal requires brief general anesthesia. Cleaning without anesthesia is superficial and can cause stress.

How often should I have my cat's teeth professionally cleaned?

Once a year is recommended for most cats. Breeds prone to tartar or with chronic oral problems may need more frequent cleanings.

Is brushing a cat's teeth at home safe?

Yes — regular brushing with a cat-specific enzymatic toothpaste and soft brush is safe. Never use human toothpaste — the fluoride it contains is toxic to cats.
