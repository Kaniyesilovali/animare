Urinary Blockage in Male Cats: The Emergency Where Hours Matter

8 July 2026 · Animare Veterinary

What Is Urinary Blockage and Why Is It So Urgent?

Urinary blockage (urethral obstruction) is the complete or partial closure of the channel that carries urine out of the bladder, caused by crystals, stones, mucus plugs or spasm. When urine cannot leave the body, the bladder overstretches, the kidneys stop working, and waste products and potassium building up in the blood disturb the heart's rhythm. An untreated complete blockage can be fatal within 24–48 hours. That is why urinary blockage is one of the true veterinary emergencies where minutes and hours matter — not something that can wait until tomorrow.

Although any cat can be affected, the condition is far more common in male cats, whose urethra is anatomically narrower. During North Cyprus's hot summer months, cats that drink too little water produce more concentrated urine, which raises the risk further.

Warning Signs: If You See This Picture, Don't Wait

  • Frequent trips to the litter box, straining for a long time but producing little or no urine
  • Crying or howling while trying to urinate, restlessness
  • Constantly licking the genital area
  • Blood in the urine, or attempts to urinate outside the box (sink, carpet)
  • As hours pass: vomiting, loss of appetite, lethargy and hiding
  • Pain when the belly is touched; a tense, full bladder

A critical warning: a cat straining in the litter box is very often assumed to be "constipated". In reality, in a large share of straining cats the problem is the urinary tract, not the bowel — and this cannot be reliably distinguished at home. Waiting it out because "it's just constipation" is the most common and most costly mistake with this disease. You can find other emergency situations in our post on the 10 signs that mean an immediate vet visit.

Which Cats Are at Risk?

  • Male cats — especially between 2 and 7 years of age
  • Neutered and overweight cats
  • Indoor-only cats with limited activity
  • Cats that drink little water and eat only dry food
  • Cats under stress — a new pet at home, moving house or a crowded household can trigger urinary tract disease

What Happens at the Clinic?

The examination starts with assessing the bladder; the diagnosis is usually made from clinical findings, urine and blood tests, and imaging when needed. In a complete blockage, the cat is sedated and the obstruction is relieved with a urinary catheter, the bladder is flushed, and intravenous fluid therapy is given to correct kidney values and potassium. Most cats are monitored in hospital for a few days. In cases that recur at short intervals, surgical options that permanently widen the urethra may be considered.

Important: pressing on your cat's belly at home to "empty the bladder" can rupture it. Oral medication or home remedies such as cranberry juice cannot open a complete blockage — they only cost precious time.

How to Prevent It from Coming Back

  • Increase water intake: Place water bowls in several spots around the house; many cats prefer running water, so a pet fountain helps. This matters even more in the hot months.
  • Add wet food to the diet: Wet food naturally raises daily water intake.
  • Urinary formula diets: For cats with a history of urinary problems, diets that regulate urine pH and mineral balance are used on veterinary advice. We covered our general approach to choosing food in our Farmina N&D pet food guide.
  • Keep the litter box clean: A dirty box makes cats hold their urine.
  • Weight and stress management: Maintaining an ideal weight and introducing household changes gradually both lower the risk.

The Bottom Line: When in Doubt, Don't Wait

If your male cat keeps visiting the litter box and cannot pass urine, this is not a wait-and-see situation. Early intervention makes treatment easier and prevents permanent kidney damage. If you notice any of these signs in your cat, get in touch with our clinic right away.

Frequently Asked Questions

My cat keeps going to the litter box but can't pee — what should I do?

This is the most typical sign of urinary blockage, especially in male cats, and it is a true emergency. Get to a veterinary clinic as soon as possible without waiting; a blockage can progress to kidney damage and become life-threatening within hours.

Which cats are most at risk of urinary blockage?

Male cats are at significantly higher risk because their urethra is narrower. Neutered, overweight, indoor-only cats, cats that drink little water or eat only dry food, and cats under stress are all in the risk group.

How can I tell urinary blockage apart from constipation?

Reliably telling them apart at home is usually not possible; in a large share of cats straining in the litter box the problem is the urinary tract, not the bowel. Waiting because your cat "looks constipated" is dangerous — the distinction is made by a clinical examination.

Can urinary blockage happen again?

Yes, cats that have had a blockage carry a risk of recurrence. Increasing water intake, adding wet food to the diet, switching to a urinary formula recommended by your veterinarian, and reducing stress all lower the risk considerably. Surgical options are considered in frequently recurring cases.
